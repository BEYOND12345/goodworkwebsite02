The Craziest Emergency Calls I've Had in Byron Bay

(And What You Can Learn From Them)

My phone rang at 2:47 AM last Sunday. "Dan, I need you here NOW - my deck just collapsed with 20 people on it!" After 15 years as a handyman in Byron Bay, I've seen it all. From midnight door lockouts with angry Airbnb guests to toilets exploding during wedding receptions (yes, really), emergency calls are just part of the job.

But here's the thing - not every "emergency" is actually an emergency, and knowing the difference can save you hundreds of dollars and a lot of stress. So let me share some of my wildest callouts and, more importantly, what you can learn from them about when to grab your tools and when to grab your phone.

SATURDAY NIGHT, 11:30 PM - SUFFOLK PARK

The Airbnb Door That Wouldn't Lock

Got a frantic call from a property manager. Four guests had just arrived from Sydney for a wedding, and the front door wouldn't lock. Not ideal when you're in a house full of strangers' expensive stuff.

When I got there, turns out the previous guests had forced the deadbolt while the door was open, bending the mechanism. The current guests were standing there with their luggage, looking pretty unimpressed.

Fixed it in 20 minutes with some adjustment and WD-40, but the property manager had already lost a 5-star review. The kicker? If they'd done basic maintenance checks between guests, this would never have happened.

What You Can Learn: Test all locks between Airbnb guests. Keep WD-40 handy. A sticky lock on Friday afternoon is an emergency callout on Saturday night.
WEDNESDAY, 3:00 AM - BYRON BAY

The Deck Collapse During a 30th Birthday

This is the one that still gives me nightmares. Old Queenslander near the Arts Estate, big party, everyone out on the deck for the speeches. CRACK! The whole thing dropped about a metre on one side.

Miraculously, nobody was seriously hurt - just some bruises and a lot of spilled drinks. When I inspected it (after making sure everyone was OK and the area was safe), the joists had rotted through where they met the posts. Classic case of water pooling where it shouldn't.

The signs had been there for months - soft spots, a slight lean, boards that flexed too much. The owner said he'd been meaning to get it checked but... you know how it goes.

What You Can Learn: If your deck feels soft or bouncy, get it checked immediately. A $500 repair could prevent a $10,000 rebuild (and potential lawsuits). I do free deck safety inspections because I've seen what happens when they fail.

How to Tell If It's a Real Emergency

Look, I get it. When something breaks in your house at 10 PM, it feels like an emergency. But after hundreds of callouts, here's my honest guide to what's actually urgent and what can wait until morning (when it won't cost you double):

Problem Is It An Emergency? What To Do
Door won't lock YES - Security issue Call immediately. Try WD-40 first
Toilet blocked Depends - Got another toilet? Try plunger first. Call if only toilet
Power out in one room Usually NO Check circuit breaker first
Water leak YES - If can't stop it Turn off water main, then call
Fence blown over Only if pool fence Secure area, call in morning
Window broken YES - Security/weather Board it up, call for proper fix
Deck damage YES - If structural Keep people off it, call immediately
SUNDAY MORNING, 7:00 AM - BANGALOW

The Toilet That Exploded During a Wedding

Wedding at a private property, 150 guests, one toilet for the granny flat where the bride was getting ready. Someone flushed, and BOOM - the cistern literally cracked in half, sending water everywhere. Bride in her dress, makeup artist's equipment soaked, absolute chaos.

The old plastic cistern had a hairline crack that finally gave way under pressure. I managed to rig up a temporary fix with some serious waterproof tape and silicone, enough to get them through the day. Came back Monday to replace the whole thing properly.

The property owner had noticed a small leak weeks earlier but figured it could wait. That "small leak" cost them a very upset bride and an emergency callout fee.

What You Can Learn: Small leaks become big problems at the worst possible time. If you see water where it shouldn't be, deal with it now. Also, keep waterproof tape in your emergency kit - it's saved many special events!

TUESDAY, MIDNIGHT - LENNOX HEAD

The Storm Damage That Couldn't Wait

During that big storm last March, got a call from an elderly couple. Tree branch had punched through their bedroom window, rain was pouring in, and they didn't know what to do.

When I got there, they'd already moved what they could, but water was pooling on their hardwood floors. We got it boarded up and tarped in about 45 minutes, saved their floors from major damage. They were so grateful they made me coffee and Tim Tams at 1 AM!

The next morning, I came back to properly fix the window. They said three other handymen hadn't answered their phones. That's the thing about emergency work - you've got to be available when people really need you.

What You Can Learn: Keep tarps and boards handy during storm season. Know how to turn off your water and power. And keep the number of someone who actually answers their phone at night!

Your Emergency Repair Kit (What I Keep in My Van)

After all these callouts, I've learned what actually helps in emergencies. Here's what you should have at home:

  • Waterproof tape - Fixes more things than you'd imagine
  • Adjustable wrench - For turning off water quickly
  • Torch and batteries - Phone lights aren't enough
  • Tarps and rope - Storm damage essential
  • WD-40 - The miracle worker for stuck locks
  • Plunger - The good kind, not the cheap one
  • Circuit breaker labels - Know what controls what

The Emergencies That Weren't

For balance, here are some of my favorite "emergency" calls that really weren't:

  • TV wall mount came loose during State of Origin (mate, just put it on a table)
  • Kitchen drawer wouldn't open with the bottle opener inside on New Year's Eve (use another drawer?)
  • Shower door came off its runner at 11 PM (use the other bathroom until morning)
  • Gate latch broke and dog might escape (bit of rope works wonders)

Don't get me wrong - I'm happy to help with all of these. But maybe not at emergency rates when a bit of creative thinking could get you through until morning!

Final Thoughts From Too Many 2 AM Callouts

After all these years and all these crazy calls, here's what I've learned: most emergencies aren't really emergencies, but when they are, you need someone reliable who'll actually show up. That's why I keep my phone on 24/7, even though my wife thinks I'm mad.

The best emergency is the one that never happens. A bit of maintenance, checking things regularly, and fixing small problems before they become big ones - that's the secret. But when things do go wrong (and they will, this is Byron Bay where salt air and humidity destroy everything), at least you know there's someone you can call.

Even at 2:47 AM on a Sunday. Just maybe check if it can wait until morning first, yeah?
