Why Your Kitchen Cupboard Doors Won't Close Properly

Northern Rivers' humidity and salt air create unique challenges for kitchen cabinetry. Here's what's really happening behind those stubborn doors.

G'day, it's Dan from Good Hands. After 15 years of fixing kitchen cupboards across Byron Bay and the Northern Rivers, I've seen every possible reason why those doors won't behave. Let me share what's really going on ‚Äì and more importantly, which fixes you can tackle yourself.

The Coastal Kitchen Challenge

Living in Byron Bay is paradise, but our 70% average humidity and salt air are absolute nightmares for kitchen cabinetry. I've fixed thousands of cupboard doors from Ballina to Tweed Heads, and I can tell you ‚Äì what works in Sydney won't cut it here.

Quick Stat:

80% of kitchen door problems in coastal homes are humidity-related. The other 20%? Usually kids hanging off them (we've all been there).

The Top 5 Culprits (Ranked by How Often I See Them)

1. Hinge Adjustment Gone Wrong (40% of calls)

Modern soft-close hinges have more adjustment points than a Swiss watch. One tiny turn of the wrong screw and suddenly your door's doing its own thing.

The DIY Test: Open the door fully. If it stays put, your hinges are probably fine. If it swings closed or open on its own, you've got an adjustment issue.

Quick Fix: Look for three screws on each hinge ‚Äì one controls up/down, one in/out, and one left/right. Start with tiny quarter turns. Most problems are fixed with the middle screw (depth adjustment).

Pro Tip:

Take a photo before adjusting anything. Trust me, you'll thank yourself later when you can't remember which way you turned what.

2. Swollen Doors from Humidity (30% of calls)

Remember that perfect-fitting door from winter? Come summer, our humidity turns it into a swollen mess. MDF and particle board are the worst offenders.

The Signs:

  • Doors fit perfectly in winter, stick in summer
  • You can see the door is physically larger than its frame
  • Paint or laminate bubbling at the edges
  • Bottom of doors near the dishwasher are especially bad

The Reality: Once MDF swells, it rarely goes back to original size. You'll need either professional door replacement or careful sanding (which removes the protective coating).

3. Worn Out Soft-Close Mechanisms (15% of calls)

Those fancy soft-close hinges that seemed so luxurious 5 years ago? They have a lifespan, especially in our salty air.

The Test: Close the door slowly. If it doesn't pull itself closed for the last inch, or if it slams instead of soft-closing, the mechanism's shot.

The Fix: Replacement only, I'm afraid. But here's the good news ‚Äì you can usually just replace the soft-close mechanism ($15) rather than the whole hinge ($40).

Warning:

Never spray WD-40 on soft-close hinges! It'll work for a day, then attract dust and salt, making them fail completely. Ask me how I learned this...

4. Cabinet Box Problems (10% of calls)

Sometimes it's not the door at all ‚Äì it's what the door's attached to. Water damage under sinks is the usual suspect.

Check These Areas:

  • Inside cabinet where hinges attach ‚Äì soft or crumbly?
  • Corner joints ‚Äì are they square?
  • Under-sink cabinets ‚Äì any signs of water damage?

5. House Movement (5% of calls)

Every house moves, but in Northern Rivers with our reactive soils, it's more dramatic. If multiple doors suddenly won't close, it might not be the kitchen's fault.

The Clue: Doors that were perfect for years suddenly all having issues, especially after very wet or dry periods.

The Fixes You Can Actually Do Yourself

For Hinge Problems:

  1. Get a proper Phillips head screwdriver (not a butter knife)
  2. Adjust one screw, one-quarter turn at a time
  3. Close door fully between adjustments
  4. Start with the middle screw on the hinge (depth)
  5. Work on one hinge at a time

For Sticky Catches:

  • Clean magnetic catches with methylated spirits
  • Adjust catch plates in or out (usually just needs loosening and sliding)
  • Replace magnetic catches entirely (they're only $3)

For Minor Swelling:

  • Run a dehumidifier in the kitchen for a week
  • Check dishwasher door seals aren't leaking steam
  • Ensure range hood actually vents outside (you'd be amazed how many don't)

When to Call for Help

Look, I'd love your business, but I'm not going to tell you to call for every little thing. However, definitely call if:

  • Multiple doors are affected (might be structural)
  • There's visible water damage or mold
  • Doors are physically damaged or split
  • You've tried adjusting but made it worse (happens to the best of us)
  • The cabinet box is damaged where hinges attach

Prevention (The Stuff Nobody Tells You)

Monthly Maintenance:

  • Wipe down hinges with a dry cloth (salt builds up invisibly)
  • Check under-sink cabinet for leaks
  • Ensure kitchen exhaust fan is actually working

Yearly Maintenance:

  • Tighten all hinge screws (they loosen with use)
  • Lubricate hinges with silicone spray (NOT WD-40)
  • Check and reseal any gaps where moisture can enter

Local Secret:

Install a small USB-powered fan inside problem cabinets. Sounds crazy, but the airflow prevents moisture buildup. I've done this for dozens of Airbnbs near the beach.

The "Should I Just Replace Them?" Question

If your doors are MDF and over 7 years old in a coastal property, replacement might be more economical than repeated repairs. Modern moisture-resistant options handle our climate much better.

  • Door adjustment service: $80-120 call out
  • New MDF door: $150-250 installed
  • Marine-grade plywood door: $300-400 installed
